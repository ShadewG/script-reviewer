# Script Shield Flagging Guide

A practical reference for true crime creators. Every example below is based on the actual rules Script Shield checks against — YouTube's Community Guidelines, Advertiser-Friendly Content Guidelines, and US defamation/privacy law.

---

## How to Read This Guide

Each section shows:

- **Flagged** — language that will trigger a warning, limit monetization, or risk removal
- **Safe** — how to say the same thing without triggering flags
- **Why** — the rule behind it

---

## 1. Naming Suspects by Case Status

The single biggest source of legal flags. What you can say depends entirely on the case status.

### Convicted

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| — | "John Smith murdered his wife in 2019." | Conviction = you can state it as fact. |
| — | "The killer lured the victim to his car." | Definitive language is fine post-conviction. |

Convicted is the safest case status. You can use direct language: "the murderer," "the killer," "committed the crime."

### Charged / On Trial

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "John Smith killed his wife." | "John Smith is **charged with** killing his wife." | He hasn't been convicted. Stating guilt as fact = defamation risk. |
| "The killer planned it for weeks." | "The **suspect allegedly** planned it for weeks." | Must attribute — "accused of," "charged with," "allegedly." |
| "She poisoned three people." | "She is **accused of** poisoning three people." | Same rule. No conviction = no definitive guilt language. |

### Suspect / Person of Interest

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "We all know Mark did it." | "Mark was **named as a person of interest** in the investigation." | Implying guilt of an uncharged person is high defamation risk. |
| "The killer is still walking free." (about a named person) | "Investigators have not made an arrest. [Name] **remains under investigation**." | Don't call someone a killer if they haven't been charged. |
| "He's guilty, the police just can't prove it." | "Questions remain about his involvement, but **no charges have been filed**." | Asserting guilt without charges = lawsuit territory. |

### Acquitted / Exonerated

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "He got away with murder." | "He was **acquitted** by a jury in 2021." | Calling an acquitted person guilty is extreme legal risk. |
| "The killer walked free." | "The jury returned a **not guilty** verdict." | Same — you cannot assert guilt after acquittal. |
| "Everyone knows she did it." | "Despite public speculation, she was **found not guilty**." | Opinion presented as consensus fact is still risky. |

## 2. Privacy and Personal Information

### Addresses

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "She lived at 1422 Maple Drive, Springfield." | "She lived in **Springfield**." | Specific street addresses are auto-flagged. Doxxing risk + YouTube privacy violation. |
| "The crime occurred at 300 Oak Avenue." | "The crime occurred at a residence in the **Westside neighborhood**." | Use city, neighborhood, or county — never the street address. |

### Minor Victims

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "The victim, Sarah Johnson, age 12..." | "The victim, **a 12-year-old girl**..." | Never name minor victims. YouTube child safety policy + legal liability. |
| "She attended Lincoln Middle School." | "She was **a middle school student in the district**." | School names can identify the child. Remove identifying details. |

### Private Medical / Financial Info

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "His medical records showed bipolar disorder and..." | "**According to court testimony**, he had a history of mental health challenges." | Private medical records of living people are protected. Only cite what's in public court records. |
| "Her bank records showed withdrawals of..." | "**Financial evidence presented at trial** showed unusual account activity." | Attribute to public proceedings; don't present private records directly. |

## 3. Graphic Content and Violence

This is the #1 factor in monetization decisions for true crime.

### Describing Injuries and Cause of Death

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "The knife severed her carotid artery, blood pooling across the kitchen floor as she bled out over several minutes." | "The victim was stabbed and died from her injuries." | Gratuitous injury detail = limited or no ads. State the fact; don't narrate the suffering. |
| "His skull was caved in, brain matter visible through the fracture." | "He died from **blunt force trauma** to the head." | Clinical language. Medical terms are fine; graphic imagery is not. |
| "She was found with her throat slit ear to ear, nearly decapitated." | "She was found dead with **fatal neck injuries**." | Summarize the wound; don't paint the picture. |

### Crime Scene Descriptions

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "Blood was splattered across every wall, the carpet soaked red, handprints smeared on the door frame." | "The crime scene showed **evidence of a violent struggle**." | Extended focus on blood/gore = no ads. One factual sentence is enough. |
| "Decomposition had set in — the smell, the insects, the bloated face..." | "The body was recovered **several weeks later** in an advanced state of decomposition." | Clinical and brief. |

### Autopsy Details

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "The autopsy revealed 37 stab wounds, each one described in detail..." | "The autopsy confirmed the **cause of death as multiple stab wounds**." | You can state findings. Don't catalog every wound. |
| "Toxicology showed she'd been drugged — GHB levels consistent with the date rape drug..." | "Toxicology results indicated the presence of **a controlled substance**." | Avoid language that reads as instructions or detail on drug effects. |

## 4. Sexual Crime Coverage

### General Rule
Use clinical, factual language. Never narrate the assault. Never describe mechanics.

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "He raped her repeatedly over several hours, forcing her to..." | "The victim reported being **sexually assaulted over an extended period**." | Detail of sexual assault = no ads + possible age restriction. |
| "The child was molested beginning at age 6. He would..." | "The abuse began when the victim was **six years old** and continued for several years." | Never describe what happened to a child victim in detail. State the fact, move on. |
| "She was found naked, tied up, with signs of sexual torture." | "The victim showed **signs of sexual assault and physical restraint**." | Clinical summary. No imagery-building language. |

## 5. Profanity

### In Video Narration

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "This case is fucked up." | "This case is deeply disturbing." | F-word in narration = limited ads. |
| "He told police, 'Go fuck yourself.'" | "He told police — and I'm quoting — '**[bleep]** yourself.'" | Quoting is okay if bleeped. Unbleeped strong profanity = limited ads. |
| "Shit" / "Bitch" / "Bastard" used repeatedly | Minimize or bleep when possible. | Moderate profanity = usually fine in narration, but heavy use degrades monetization. |

### In Titles and Thumbnails

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "The F*cking Truth About the Johnson Case" | "The Disturbing Truth About the Johnson Case" | ANY profanity in title = no ads. Even censored versions (f*ck, sh*t). |
| Thumbnail with profanity text | Thumbnail with case imagery + clean text | Same rule — profanity in thumbnails = no ads, no exceptions, no EDSA override. |

---

## 6. Hate Speech and Slurs

### Covering Hate Crimes

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "He called them [racial slur] before opening fire." | "He used **racial slurs** before opening fire." | Don't reproduce the slur — even in direct quotes. Paraphrase or bleep. |
| Presenting perpetrator's ideology without editorial framing | "The perpetrator was motivated by white supremacist ideology — **a hateful and false worldview** that..." | You MUST editorially reject the ideology. Documentary coverage requires condemnation. |
| "These people are animals." (about any group) | Remove entirely, or reframe: "The perpetrator dehumanized the victims, calling them..." | Dehumanizing language about any group = hate speech flag, even if quoting someone. |

## 7. Thumbnails and Titles

### Thumbnails

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| Unblurred crime scene photo as main image | Mugshot + case title, news-style layout | Graphic imagery in thumbnails = limited/no ads. |
| Photo of dead body (even partially blurred) | Courtroom sketch, location photo, or stylized graphic | Dead bodies in thumbnails are nearly always flagged. |
| Victim photo used in shocking/exploitative way | Victim photo with respectful, news-style framing | Intent matters — journalism vs. shock value. |

### Titles

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "SHOCKING: The Most BRUTAL Murder Ever Caught on Camera" | "The Investigation Into the 2019 Springfield Case" | Clickbait + shock language in titles degrades monetization and risks spam flags. |
| "He KILLED Her and NOBODY Cared" | "The Unsolved Disappearance of Jane Doe" | Caps lock + emotional manipulation reads as engagement bait. |
| Title promising "exclusive evidence" that's not in the video | Title accurately describing what the video covers | Misleading titles violate spam/deceptive practices policy. |

## 8. EDSA Context (Educational, Documentary, Scientific, Artistic)

EDSA is YouTube's exception that lets true crime creators cover dark subjects. But it has rules.

### What Counts as EDSA Context

- Educational/documentary purpose is clear **in the video and audio itself** (title and description alone don't count)
- Narration provides journalistic framing, not just recounting events
- Graphic content serves the story — it's not the focal point
- You condemn violence/hate when covering those topics
- You provide resources where appropriate (crisis hotlines, etc.)

### What Doesn't Count

| Not EDSA | Why |
|----------|-----|
| "Here's the crime scene footage" with no narration | No editorial context in the video itself. |
| Showing graphic autopsy photos for shock value | Graphic content as focal point, not supporting educational narrative. |
| Extended focus on perpetrator's manifesto without editorial rejection | Presenting extremist content without condemnation. |
| Listing bomb-making materials "for educational purposes" | Instructions for dangerous acts are never protected by EDSA. |

## 9. Suicide and Self-Harm

When a case involves suicide (perpetrator or victim):

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "He hanged himself in his cell using a bedsheet." | "He **died by suicide** while in custody." | Never specify method. YouTube's safe messaging guidelines. |
| "She took a handful of pills and..." | "She **took her own life** on March 15th." | Same rule — method specification is flagged. |
| Romanticizing: "He couldn't live with the guilt, it consumed him until..." | "Following his conviction, he died by suicide." | Don't romanticize or build narrative around suicidal ideation. State the fact. |

## 10. Organized Crime and Terrorism

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| Extended focus on cartel "power" and "empire" | "The cartel operated through violence and intimidation, **resulting in hundreds of deaths**." | Don't glorify. Focus on impact and harm, not power. |
| Quoting terrorist manifestos without editorial framing | "The perpetrator published a manifesto. **Its claims are false and hateful.** Investigators found that..." | You can reference manifestos but must editorially reject the ideology. |
| Showing terrorist organization logos/flags prominently | Brief display with educational context, or describe verbally without showing | Prominent display without condemnation = promoting the organization. |

---

## 11. Firearms

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "Here's how an AR-15 can be modified to fire automatically..." | "The weapon used was **a semi-automatic rifle**." | Never provide modification or manufacturing instructions. |
| "You can buy a ghost gun kit at..." | "The firearm was **untraceable**, commonly referred to as a ghost gun." | Don't link to or describe how to purchase weapons. |
| Detailed breakdown of how a specific weapon was built | "**Ballistics confirmed** the weapon was a .45 caliber handgun." | State what was used. Don't explain how to build or modify it. |

---

## 12. Drug-Related Content

| Flagged | Safe | Why |
|---------|------|-----|
| "Here's how methamphetamine is cooked..." | "The suspect operated a **methamphetamine production operation**." | Never provide manufacturing instructions. |
| Extended profile of cartel leader glorifying their lifestyle | "The cartel was responsible for trafficking **an estimated $2 billion** in narcotics, leading to thousands of deaths." | Focus on harm and criminal impact, not lifestyle. |
| "The dark web marketplace where they bought fentanyl was..." | "Investigators traced the purchase to **an illegal online marketplace**." | Never name specific marketplaces or provide access instructions. |

## Quick Reference Card

### Always Safe
- Convicted person + definitive language ("the killer," "murdered")
- Clinical cause of death ("died from blunt force trauma")
- City/county locations (never street addresses)
- "Accused of" / "charged with" for non-convicted
- Bleeped profanity in narration
- Age-only references for minor victims ("a 14-year-old")

### Always Flagged
- Street addresses (auto-detected)
- Strong profanity in titles or thumbnails
- Naming minor victims
- Calling acquitted people guilty
- Graphic injury detail beyond clinical summary
- Suicide method specification
- Weapon modification instructions
- Drug manufacturing details

### Monetization Tiers

| Content | Expected Monetization |
|---------|----------------------|
| Factual narration, no graphic imagery, proper attribution | Full Ads |
| Crime scene photos (blurred) + documentary narration | Limited Ads |
| Graphic injury descriptions, moderate profanity throughout | Limited Ads |
| Strong profanity in title, unblurred graphic imagery, detailed violence | No Ads |
| Naming minor victims, doxxing addresses, glorifying violence | Removal Risk |

---

## The Golden Rule

**State the facts. Don't paint the picture.**

"She was stabbed and died from her injuries" tells the viewer what happened.
"The knife plunged into her chest as she screamed" tries to make the viewer feel it.

The first is journalism. The second is entertainment shock value. YouTube and the law treat them very differently.
